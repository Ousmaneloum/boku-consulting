The State of Payments in the WAEMU Zone

The West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) encompasses eight francophone nations sharing the CFA franc under the monetary authority of the Banque Centrale des États de l'Afrique de l'Ouest (BCEAO). Despite sharing a common currency for over 50 years, the region's payment infrastructure remains deeply fragmented — a paradox that represents both a significant economic inefficiency and an extraordinary opportunity for Digital Public Infrastructure investment.

The core paradox: Citizens of Sénégal and Côte d'Ivoire share a currency, a central bank, and a common economic policy — yet a merchant in Dakar cannot accept payment from a customer in Abidjan without converting through correspondent banking channels that add cost, delay, and friction that cash does not.

Why Interoperability Has Failed So Far

Multiple attempts at payment interoperability in the WAEMU zone have stalled or produced limited results. Understanding why is essential to designing an approach that works:

  • Commercial incentives misaligned: Mobile money operators profit from network effects — keeping users within their platform. Interoperability by definition reduces this advantage, creating rational resistance from private sector players.
  • Regulatory fragmentation: Despite BCEAO oversight, national regulatory authorities have implemented payment regulations inconsistently, creating legal ambiguity for cross-border transactions.
  • Technical standards divergence: Each operator has built proprietary APIs, transaction formats, and reconciliation systems — making technical integration expensive and slow.
  • Identity layer gaps: Without a common digital identity foundation, cross-border payment authorisation requires manual KYC re-verification that breaks the user experience.

The DPI Approach: A Layered Architecture

The solution is not a single unified payment platform — a model that has failed repeatedly in Africa due to procurement complexity and operator resistance. Instead, Boku Consulting advocates for a layered Digital Public Infrastructure approach that separates public goods from private competition:

L4 Applications Private sector wallets, merchant apps, government disbursement systems — competing on user experience
L3 Interoperability Switch A BCEAO-governed clearing and settlement layer — the "public road" that all private payment vehicles use
L2 Identity & Trust Federated digital identity verification enabling cross-border KYC without repeated re-verification
L1 Legal & Regulatory Harmonised BCEAO payment regulations, AML/CFT standards, and cross-border data sharing frameworks

The Critical Role of Layer 3: The Interoperability Switch

The centrepiece of a functional WAEMU payment interoperability system must be a BCEAO-governed switch — a neutral technical intermediary that routes transactions between operators, manages clearing, and performs net settlement. This model, successfully implemented in India (UPI), Tanzania (TIPS), and Ghana (GhIPSS), removes the commercial conflict from interoperability by making the rails a public good.

Lessons from Comparable Implementations

Ghana: GhIPSS as a Model

Ghana's interbank payment switch, operated by the Ghana Interbank Payment and Settlement Systems (GhIPSS), demonstrates that a government-owned neutral switch can achieve high adoption. The Mobile Money Interoperability (MMI) system launched in 2018 now processes over 40 million interoperability transactions monthly.

Tanzania: The TIPS Model

Tanzania's payment interoperability framework, mandated by the Bank of Tanzania, achieved full mobile money interoperability between all major operators within 18 months of regulatory mandate — demonstrating that central bank authority, when exercised decisively, can overcome commercial resistance.

Priority Recommendations for BCEAO and Member States

  1. Issue a BCEAO directive mandating payment interoperability participation for all licensed mobile money operators — with a 24-month implementation timeline
  2. Establish a WAEMU Payment Switch Authority as a subsidiary of BCEAO to govern the neutral interoperability infrastructure
  3. Harmonise AML/CFT transaction monitoring standards across all 8 member states to remove cross-border compliance barriers
  4. Invest in a federated digital identity layer — leveraging existing national ID infrastructure — to enable seamless cross-border KYC
  5. Create a WAEMU API standards body to mandate common technical interfaces for all licensed payment service providers
