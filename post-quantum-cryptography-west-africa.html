Executive Summary

The emergence of cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) represents one of the most consequential threats to digital public infrastructure ever documented. Unlike conventional cyberattacks, the quantum threat does not require a breach today — adversaries are harvesting encrypted data now, with the intent to decrypt it once sufficiently powerful quantum hardware becomes available.

For West African governments managing citizen registries, financial transaction records, diplomatic communications, and national security data, this creates an urgent and non-negotiable mandate: begin post-quantum cryptography (PQC) migration planning immediately, regardless of whether quantum computers are commercially available today.

The Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL) threat: Nation-state actors and sophisticated criminal organisations are systematically capturing encrypted government data today, storing it, and waiting for quantum computing capability to mature — estimated by leading research institutions to be 8–15 years away at scale.

The Threat Landscape for West Africa

West African governments face a unique combination of factors that amplify the quantum threat relative to more technologically mature regions:

  • Accelerated digitalisation without legacy security debt: Many ECOWAS nations are deploying large-scale digital ID, payment, and registry systems for the first time — creating windows to embed quantum-resistant standards from the outset, but also concentrating sensitive data in newly built repositories.
  • Geopolitical targeting: Natural resource wealth, strategic port infrastructure, and growing diplomatic influence make West African state systems increasingly attractive targets for sophisticated nation-state actors.
  • Long data sensitivity windows: Civil registration data, land title records, and judicial documents retain sensitivity for decades — well within the quantum threat timeline.
  • Interoperability dependencies: ECOWAS cross-border systems for identity verification and payments create shared cryptographic surfaces that, if compromised, affect multiple sovereign states simultaneously.

NIST Post-Quantum Standards: What Governments Need to Know

In August 2024, the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) finalised the first set of post-quantum cryptographic standards — a landmark moment that effectively marks the starting gun for global migration programmes.

The Three Finalised Standards

  • CRYSTALS-Kyber (ML-KEM): For key encapsulation — replacing RSA and Diffie-Hellman in secure communications
  • CRYSTALS-Dilithium (ML-DSA): For digital signatures — replacing ECDSA in document signing and authentication
  • SPHINCS+ (SLH-DSA): Hash-based signature scheme providing a conservative, well-understood alternative

Recommendation for ECOWAS governments: Any new digital infrastructure procurement — including identity systems, payment gateways, and data exchange platforms — should specify PQC algorithm support as a mandatory tender requirement from 2025 onwards.

Migration Roadmap Framework

Phase 1: Cryptographic Inventory (Months 1–6)

Identify all cryptographic assets across government systems — certificates, key management systems, VPNs, digital signature workflows, and data-at-rest encryption. Most ECOWAS governments will discover hundreds of discrete cryptographic dependencies.

Phase 2: Risk Prioritisation (Months 4–9)

Classify data by sensitivity duration. Records that must remain confidential beyond 10 years require immediate prioritisation. Financial transaction logs, biometric databases, and diplomatic communications typically fall into this category.

Phase 3: Hybrid Deployment (Months 8–24)

Deploy hybrid classical/post-quantum cryptography — combining existing algorithms (AES-256, ECDSA) with new PQC standards. This provides protection against both conventional and quantum attacks during the transition period.

Immediate Actions for Government CIOs

  1. Commission a cryptographic inventory of all government systems within 90 days
  2. Establish a cross-ministry PQC working group including IT, legal, and procurement leads
  3. Insert PQC readiness requirements into all active and upcoming IT procurement tenders
  4. Engage with ECOWAS cyber-security bodies to coordinate a regional approach
  5. Prioritise migration of citizen biometric databases and civil registry systems
